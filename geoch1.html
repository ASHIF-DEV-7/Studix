What are Resources?

Definition: Resources are substances or things that have utility and value for human beings. Everything in our environment which can be used to satisfy our needs and is technologically accessible, economically feasible, and culturally acceptable is termed as a resource.

Characteristics of Resources:

  • Utility: Resources must have some use or value
  • Limited Quantity: Resources are available in limited quantities
  • Economic Value: Resources have economic importance
  • Technology Dependent: Availability depends on technology

Resource Formation:

A substance becomes a resource when:

  • It has some utility to satisfy human needs
  • Technology is available to access and use it
  • It is economically viable to extract and use
  • It is culturally acceptable in society
Example: Water in a well becomes a resource when there is technology (pump) to extract it, it's economically viable, and people accept its use.

Classification of Resources

1. On the basis of Origin

A) Biotic Resources

  • Resources obtained from living organisms
  • Examples: Forests, wildlife, fisheries, livestock, human beings
  • Renewable in nature if used sustainably

B) Abiotic Resources

  • Resources obtained from non-living things
  • Examples: Minerals, rocks, metals, fossil fuels
  • Generally non-renewable in nature

2. On the basis of Exhaustibility

A) Renewable Resources

  • Resources that can be renewed or reproduced
  • Can be used repeatedly without exhausting
  • Examples: Solar energy, wind energy, water, forests
  • Need careful management to maintain sustainability

B) Non-renewable Resources

  • Resources that cannot be renewed or reproduced
  • Get exhausted with continuous use
  • Examples: Coal, petroleum, natural gas, minerals
  • Take millions of years to form

3. On the basis of Ownership

Type Description Examples Management
Individual Resources Owned by private individuals House, car, plantation, pond Managed by individuals
Community Resources Accessible to all community members Grazing grounds, burial grounds, parks Community management
National Resources Owned by the nation Roads, railways, minerals, water resources Government control
International Resources Beyond national boundaries Oceanic resources beyond 200 nautical miles International institutions

4. On the basis of Status of Development

A) Potential Resources

  • Resources that exist but not yet developed
  • Technology not available or economically not viable
  • Examples: Wind energy in Rajasthan, solar energy in Gujarat

B) Developed Resources

  • Resources that have been surveyed and developed
  • Quality and quantity determined
  • Currently being used

C) Stock

  • Materials that have potential but lack technology
  • Cannot be used at present
  • Example: Hydrogen in water for energy

D) Reserves

  • Subset of stock that can be developed
  • Technology available but not being used
  • Kept for future use

Resource Planning

Resource Planning: It is a strategy for judicious use of resources to avoid wastage and ensure sustainable development for present and future generations.

Need for Resource Planning:

  • Uneven Distribution: Resources are unevenly distributed across regions
  • Population Pressure: Increasing population puts pressure on resources
  • Wasteful Usage: Irrational consumption and over-utilization
  • Environmental Degradation: Need to prevent environmental damage
  • Sustainable Development: Ensure resources for future generations

Steps in Resource Planning:

Step 1: Survey and Mapping

Identification and inventory of resources across different regions using surveys, maps, and remote sensing.

Step 2: Evolving Planning Structure

Creating appropriate technology, skill, and institutional setup for implementing resource development plans.

Step 3: Resource Development Plan

Matching resource development plans with overall national development plans considering environmental and social factors.

Problems in Resource Planning:

  • Lack of proper survey and mapping
  • Inadequate technology and skilled manpower
  • Lack of coordination between different agencies
  • Political and administrative hurdles
  • Environmental constraints
  • Financial constraints
Important: India has enormous resource potential but lacks proper planning and management. This leads to regional imbalances and environmental degradation.

Land Resources

Importance of Land:

  • Supports natural vegetation, wildlife, and human life
  • Provides space for agriculture, housing, and industries
  • Source of minerals and other resources
  • Basis for economic activities

Land Utilization in India:

Land Use Category Percentage of Total Land Description
Forest 22.5% Land under forest cover
Agricultural Land 51% Net sown area and fallow lands
Pasture and Grazing 3.3% Land used for grazing livestock
Miscellaneous Uses 7.2% Settlements, roads, industries
Wasteland 16% Barren, rocky, desert land

Physical Factors Affecting Land Use:

  • Topography: Mountainous areas less suitable for agriculture
  • Climate: Temperature and rainfall affect land use
  • Soil Type: Different crops need different soil types
  • Water Availability: Determines irrigation possibilities

Human Factors Affecting Land Use:

  • Population Density: High density leads to intensive land use
  • Technology: Advanced technology enables better land use
  • Economic Factors: Market demand and capital availability
  • Transportation: Good transport increases land value
Note: India has only 2.4% of world's land area but supports 17.5% of world's population, creating pressure on land resources.

Soil as a Resource

Soil: The thin layer of grainy substance covering the rock surface of the earth is called soil. It is formed by weathering of rocks over thousands of years.

Soil Formation Process:

Soil formation is a slow process that takes thousands of years. It involves:

  • Physical Weathering: Breaking of rocks by temperature, wind, rain
  • Chemical Weathering: Chemical breakdown of rock minerals
  • Biological Activity: Action of plants, animals, and microorganisms
  • Organic Matter Addition: Decomposition of plant and animal remains

Factors Affecting Soil Formation:

  • Parent Rock: Type of rock determines soil characteristics
  • Climate: Temperature and rainfall affect weathering rate
  • Topography: Slope affects soil depth and erosion
  • Time: Longer time allows deeper soil formation
  • Organisms: Plants and animals contribute to soil formation

Importance of Soil:

  • Medium for plant growth and agriculture
  • Supports natural vegetation and ecosystems
  • Filters and purifies water
  • Stores carbon and nutrients
  • Provides raw materials for construction

Major Soil Types in India:

Soil Type Characteristics Distribution Suitable Crops
Alluvial Soil Highly fertile, rich in potash Northern plains, coastal areas Rice, wheat, cotton, sugarcane
Black Soil Rich in lime, iron, alumina Deccan plateau Cotton, sugarcane
Red and Yellow Soil Rich in iron oxide Eastern and southern plateau Rice, ragi, groundnut
Laterite Soil Rich in iron and aluminium Heavy rainfall areas Tea, coffee, cashew

Resource Conservation

Need for Conservation:

  • Limited Resources: Most resources are limited in quantity
  • Growing Population: Increasing demand due to population growth
  • Environmental Protection: Prevent environmental degradation
  • Sustainable Development: Meet present needs without compromising future
  • Economic Benefits: Reduce costs and increase efficiency

Methods of Resource Conservation:

Land Conservation:

  • Afforestation and reforestation
  • Proper land use planning
  • Preventing soil erosion
  • Organic farming practices
  • Crop rotation and fallowing

Soil Conservation:

  • Contour Plowing: Plowing along slope contours
  • Terrace Farming: Creating step-like fields on slopes
  • Strip Cropping: Alternating strips of different crops
  • Shelter Belts: Planting trees as windbreaks
  • Mulching: Covering soil with organic matter

Water Conservation:

  • Rainwater harvesting
  • Watershed management
  • Drip irrigation
  • Preventing water pollution
  • Recycling and reusing water
Success Story: The Chipko Movement in Uttarakhand successfully prevented deforestation and promoted forest conservation through community participation.

Government Initiatives:

  • National Forest Policy: Aims to maintain 33% forest cover
  • Soil Conservation Programs: Watershed development schemes
  • Environmental Laws: Forest Conservation Act, Water Act
  • Afforestation Programs: Van Mahotsav, Green India Mission
  • Sustainable Agriculture: Promoting organic farming
Remember: Resource conservation is not just government responsibility but requires participation from every individual and community.

Individual's Role in Conservation:

  • Reduce, reuse, and recycle materials
  • Use renewable energy sources
  • Practice water and energy conservation
  • Support sustainable products
  • Participate in community conservation programs
  • Spread awareness about conservation

Key Points to Remember

  • Resources are vital for human survival and development
  • Proper classification helps in better resource management
  • Resource planning is essential for sustainable development
  • Land and soil are precious resources that need conservation
  • Conservation requires both individual and collective efforts
  • Technology plays a crucial role in resource development
  • Environmental protection is key to resource sustainability
