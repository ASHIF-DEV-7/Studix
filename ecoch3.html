3.1 Introduction to Money

What is Money?

Money is a medium of exchange that facilitates trade and commerce. It eliminates the need for barter system and serves as a store of value, unit of account, and standard of deferred payment.

Evolution of Money

  1. Barter System: Direct exchange of goods for goods
  2. Commodity Money: Use of commodities like gold, silver as medium of exchange
  3. Metallic Money: Standardized coins made of precious metals
  4. Paper Money: Currency notes backed by government guarantee
  5. Plastic Money: Credit cards, debit cards
  6. Digital Money: Online transactions, mobile banking

Functions of Money

Primary Functions:

  • Medium of Exchange: Facilitates trade by eliminating double coincidence of wants
  • Measure of Value: Common unit to measure and compare values of goods and services

Secondary Functions:

  • Store of Value: Money can be saved for future use
  • Standard of Deferred Payment: Basis for credit transactions

3.2 Modern Forms of Money

Currency

Currency includes paper notes and coins issued by the central bank (Reserve Bank of India). It is accepted as a medium of exchange because it is authorized by the government.

Deposits with Banks

People deposit their money in banks for safety and to earn interest. These deposits can be withdrawn on demand and are considered as money.

Types of Deposits:

  • Demand Deposits: Can be withdrawn at any time (Current Account, Savings Account)
  • Time Deposits: Cannot be withdrawn before maturity (Fixed Deposits)

Example:

Rohan has ₹10,000 in his savings account. He can withdraw this money anytime using a cheque or debit card. This deposit acts as money because it can be used for transactions.

Cheques

Cheque:

A cheque is a paper instructing the bank to pay a specific amount from the person's account to the person in whose name the cheque has been issued.

3.3 Banking System in India

Role of Banks

  1. Accept Deposits: Banks accept money from people as deposits
  2. Provide Loans: Banks lend money to borrowers who need funds
  3. Payment Services: Facilitate payments through cheques, cards, online transfers
  4. Foreign Exchange: Convert one currency to another

Types of Banks

Type Examples Characteristics
Commercial Banks SBI, HDFC, ICICI Accept deposits, provide loans, general banking
Cooperative Banks Urban Cooperative Banks Serve specific communities or regions
Regional Rural Banks Gramin Banks Focus on rural areas and agriculture

Reserve Bank of India (RBI)

Functions of RBI:

  • Issues currency notes and coins
  • Controls and supervises other banks
  • Implements monetary policy
  • Acts as banker to the government
  • Manages foreign exchange reserves

3.4 How Banks Work

Bank's Business Model

Bank's Profit = Interest Earned on Loans - Interest Paid on Deposits - Operating Expenses

Process:

  1. Banks accept deposits from customers
  2. Pay interest on these deposits (usually 3-6% annually)
  3. Lend this money to borrowers at higher interest rates (8-15% annually)
  4. Keep some money as cash reserves for daily transactions
  5. The difference between lending rate and deposit rate is bank's income

Example:

ABC Bank collects ₹100 crores as deposits and pays 4% interest = ₹4 crores. It lends ₹80 crores at 12% interest = ₹9.6 crores. Bank's gross income = ₹9.6 - ₹4 = ₹5.6 crores.

Cash Reserve Ratio (CRR)

Banks must keep a certain percentage of their deposits as cash reserves with RBI. Currently, CRR is around 4%. This ensures banks have enough liquidity for withdrawals.

3.5 Credit and Loans

What is Credit?

Credit:

Credit refers to an agreement where the borrower receives money or resources with the promise to repay the lender in the future, usually with interest.

Terms of Credit

  1. Interest Rate: Extra money to be paid along with repayment
  2. Collateral: Asset owned by borrower used as guarantee
  3. Documentation: Proof of employment, income, identity
  4. Mode of Repayment: How and when loan will be repaid

Example of Terms of Credit:

Priya wants a loan of ₹2 lakh for her small business:

  • Interest Rate: 10% per annum
  • Collateral: Her shop as security
  • Documentation: Business license, income proof, identity proof
  • Repayment: Monthly installments of ₹18,000 for 12 months

Types of Loans

3.6 Formal and Informal Credit

Formal Sources of Credit

Characteristics:

  • Regulated by RBI
  • Lower interest rates
  • Proper documentation
  • Legal protection for borrowers

Examples:

Commercial banks, Cooperative banks, Regional Rural Banks, Government schemes

Informal Sources of Credit

Characteristics:

  • Not regulated by RBI
  • Higher interest rates
  • Minimal documentation
  • Quick approval process

Examples:

Moneylenders, Traders, Employers, Relatives, Friends, Self-Help Groups

Comparison

Aspect Formal Credit Informal Credit
Interest Rate 8-15% annually 24-60% annually
Documentation Extensive Minimal
Collateral Required for large loans May or may not be required
Time for Approval 7-30 days Immediate to 1 week

3.7 Self-Help Groups (SHGs)

Self-Help Groups:

Small groups of 15-20 people from similar economic backgrounds who save regularly and lend to members in need. They bridge the gap between formal and informal credit.

How SHGs Work

  1. Group members save small amounts regularly (₹50-100 per month)
  2. Money is pooled together in a common fund
  3. Members can borrow from this fund for various needs
  4. Interest charged is much lower than moneylenders
  5. After establishing good track record, banks provide loans to the group

Advantages of SHGs

  • Easy access to credit for poor people
  • Lower interest rates compared to informal sources
  • Develops saving habit
  • Women empowerment
  • No collateral required
  • Flexible repayment terms

Example:

Meera joins a SHG with 19 other women. Each saves ₹100 monthly. After 6 months, the group has ₹12,000. When Meera needs ₹5,000 for her daughter's education, she borrows from the group at 12% annual interest instead of going to a moneylender who charges 36%.

3.8 Problems with Informal Credit

High Interest Rates

Informal lenders charge very high interest rates, sometimes 3-5% per month (36-60% annually), which can trap borrowers in debt cycles.

Unfair Practices

  • No proper documentation of loan terms
  • Harassment for repayment
  • Seizure of collateral without proper legal process
  • Compound interest calculations

Debt Trap

When high interest rates make it impossible for borrowers to repay loans, they are forced to take more loans to repay existing ones, creating a debt trap.

3.9 Government Initiatives

Financial Inclusion Programs

  • Jan Dhan Yojana: Bank accounts for all citizens
  • Mudra Yojana: Small loans for micro-enterprises
  • Kisan Credit Card: Easy credit for farmers
  • Priority Sector Lending: Banks must lend 40% to agriculture and small industries

Digital Payment Systems

  • UPI (Unified Payments Interface)
  • Mobile Banking
  • Digital wallets (Paytm, PhonePe, etc.)
  • NEFT/RTGS for large transactions

3.10 Important Terms and Definitions

  • Double Coincidence of Wants: Both parties in barter must want what the other has to offer
  • Legal Tender: Currency that must be accepted as payment by law
  • Liquidity: How easily an asset can be converted to cash
  • Collateral: Asset pledged as security for a loan
  • Default: Failure to repay a loan as agreed
  • Credit Rating: Assessment of borrower's ability to repay loans
  • Financial Inclusion: Making financial services accessible to all

3.11 Key Points to Remember

  1. Money eliminates the problem of double coincidence of wants in barter system
  2. Modern money includes currency and bank deposits
  3. Banks act as intermediaries between savers and borrowers
  4. RBI is the central bank that controls the banking system
  5. Formal credit sources are better than informal ones due to lower interest rates
  6. Self-Help Groups provide an alternative for people excluded from formal banking
  7. High interest rates in informal credit can lead to debt traps
  8. Government initiatives aim to increase financial inclusion
