Introduction to Forest and Wildlife Resources

What are Forest and Wildlife Resources?

Forest and wildlife resources are renewable natural resources that provide essential services to humanity and maintain ecological balance. These resources are vital for:

  • Maintaining biodiversity and ecosystem stability
  • Providing raw materials for various industries
  • Supporting livelihoods of millions of people
  • Climate regulation and carbon sequestration
  • Soil and water conservation
Key Point: India has about 21% of its total land area under forests and supports 8% of the world's biodiversity despite having only 2.4% of the world's land area.

Biodiversity and Conservation

Types of Biodiversity

Genetic Diversity Diversity in genes within a species, enabling adaptation to changing environments.
Species Diversity Variety of species in a given ecosystem or on Earth as a whole.
Ecosystem Diversity Variety of ecosystems present in a region or biosphere.

Flora and Fauna

Flora: Plant species found in a particular region

Fauna: Animal species found in a particular region

India's Rich Biodiversity:
  • 47,000 plant species (10% of world's flora)
  • 89,000 animal species (6.5% of world's fauna)
  • 15,000 flowering plants (6% of world total)

Forest Types in India

1. Reserved Forests

  • More than half of total forest land (54%)
  • Most valuable forests from conservation perspective
  • No rights for local communities
  • Strict protection and management

2. Protected Forests

  • About one-third of total forest area (29%)
  • Forest department has limited rights
  • Some community access allowed
  • Buffer zones around reserved forests

3. Unclassed Forests

  • Remaining forest land (17%)
  • Belongs to government and private individuals
  • Community access for grazing, fuel collection
  • Least protected category

Threats to Forest and Wildlife

Major Threats

  • Deforestation: Clearing forests for agriculture, urbanization, and industrial development
  • Forest Fires: Natural and human-induced fires destroying vast forest areas
  • Overgrazing: Excessive grazing by livestock damaging forest ecosystems
  • Mining: Extraction of minerals destroying forest habitats
  • Pollution: Industrial and agricultural pollution affecting forest health
  • Poaching: Illegal hunting of wildlife for commercial purposes
Alarming Statistics: India loses about 1.38 million hectares of forest cover annually due to various human activities.

Conservation Strategies

In-situ Conservation

Conservation of species in their natural habitat:

  • National Parks: 104 national parks covering 40,501 sq km
  • Wildlife Sanctuaries: 544 sanctuaries covering 118,918 sq km
  • Biosphere Reserves: 18 reserves covering 54,716 sq km

Ex-situ Conservation

Conservation outside natural habitat:

  • Zoological gardens and botanical gardens
  • Seed banks and gene banks
  • Tissue culture and cryopreservation
  • Breeding programs for endangered species
Project Tiger Launched in 1973 to protect the endangered Bengal tiger and its habitat.
Project Elephant Started in 1992 to protect elephants and their corridors.
Project Rhinoceros Conservation program for the one-horned rhinoceros in Assam.

Community Participation

Joint Forest Management (JFM)

A partnership between forest department and local communities for sustainable forest management:

  • Local communities protect and manage forest resources
  • Benefits shared between community and forest department
  • Emphasis on sustainable use of forest products
  • Successful in states like Odisha, Haryana, and West Bengal

Chipko Movement

Famous environmental movement in Uttarakhand (then Uttar Pradesh):

  • Started in 1973 by Sunderlal Bahuguna
  • Villagers hugged trees to prevent cutting
  • Raised awareness about deforestation
  • Led to policy changes in forest management

Chapter Summary

Key Takeaways

  • Forests and wildlife are renewable resources crucial for ecological balance
  • India has rich biodiversity but faces serious conservation challenges
  • Three types of forests: Reserved, Protected, and Unclassed
  • Major threats include deforestation, pollution, and poaching
  • Conservation through in-situ and ex-situ methods
  • Community participation essential for sustainable management
  • Government initiatives like Project Tiger show positive results
Remember: Conservation is not just the government's responsibility but requires active participation from all citizens to ensure sustainable development.
