CBSE Class 10 SST PYQs

Previous Year Questions from 2022 & 2023 - A Millennium-style study guide for distraction-free learning.

History

Q1. How did the First World War create a new political and economic situation in India? (2023)

The war led to a sharp increase in **defense expenditure**, financed by war loans and increased taxes. This created an **inflationary period** with rising prices. Forced recruitment in rural areas caused widespread anger. This new situation provided the context for the growth of nationalist movements.

Q2. What was the impact of the Great Depression on the Indian economy? (2023)

The Great Depression severely affected the Indian economy. **Agricultural prices plummeted**, and exports declined. The colonial government continued to demand high revenue, forcing farmers to sell their land. This widespread distress fueled the nationalist struggle.

Q3. Explain the different stages of the Non-Cooperation Movement. (2022)

The movement began with the **surrender of titles** and honorary posts. Following this, a **boycott of civil services**, army, police, schools, and foreign goods was announced. In the later stages, it included the picketing of liquor shops and a boycott of foreign clothes. It was eventually withdrawn after the Chauri Chaura incident in 1922.

Q4. How did the growth of nationalism in Europe lead to the formation of nation-states? (2023)

Nationalism in Europe transformed from loyalty to a monarch to a belief in a shared identity. This led to movements for **unification and independence**, such as the unification of Germany and Italy. People demanded a state where they could live as a cohesive community with shared values, leading to the creation of new nation-states.

Q5. Who was Giuseppe Mazzini and what was his contribution to the unification of Italy? (2023)

Giuseppe Mazzini was an Italian revolutionary and a founder of 'Young Italy'. He believed in a unified Italian Republic. His ideas inspired subsequent generations of nationalists. He is considered the 'spiritual force' behind the unification of Italy, as his vision fueled the movement.

Q6. Explain the role of women in the nationalist struggles in India. (2022)

Women played a significant role by actively participating in protests, marches, and civil disobedience campaigns. Figures like Sarojini Naidu and Annie Besant mobilized other women and contributed to the movement. Their involvement challenged traditional gender roles and broadened the base of the freedom struggle.

Q7. Describe the Dandi March. (2023)

The Dandi March was a significant act of **civil disobedience** led by Mahatma Gandhi. On March 12, 1930, he began the march from his Sabarmati Ashram to the coastal town of Dandi to **violate the British salt law**. The march covered over 240 miles and sparked widespread civil disobedience across India.

Q8. How did industrialization affect society in 19th-century Europe? (2022)

Industrialization led to massive social changes. It created new social classes, such as the **industrial working class and the middle class**. It also led to rapid **urbanization** and problems like crowded housing and poor sanitation, which gave rise to socialist and labor movements.

Q9. What was the impact of the Rowlatt Act on the people of India? (2022)

The Rowlatt Act gave the government enormous powers to **repress political activities** and allowed detention of political prisoners without trial for two years. The people of India protested against this act through a nationwide hartal, rallies, and strikes. This led to the Jallianwala Bagh massacre.

Q10. What role did the Ghadar Party and the Hindustan Republican Association play in the Indian freedom struggle? (2022)

The Ghadar Party was a revolutionary group formed by Indians in the United States and Canada to overthrow British rule. The Hindustan Republican Association was an Indian revolutionary organization founded by Ram Prasad Bismil and others to fight for independence through armed revolution.

Political Science

Q11. Explain the concept of 'power sharing.' Why is it desirable? (2023)

Power sharing is a system where power is shared among different organs and levels of government, and different social groups. It is desirable because it **reduces conflict** between social groups, ensures **stability**, and upholds the spirit of democracy.

Q12. How is a 'federal system' different from a 'unitary system'? (2023)

In a **federal system**, power is divided between a central authority and constituent units, with both having their own jurisdiction. In a **unitary system**, there is only one level of government, or sub-units are subordinate to the central government. India is federal, while the UK is unitary.

Q13. What are the key features of the federal system in India? (2022)

Key features include a **two-tier system** of government (Union and State), a **written constitution**, the **division of powers** between the Union and State lists, and an **independent judiciary** to resolve disputes. The constitution is supreme, and its provisions can only be changed with the consent of both levels of government.

Q14. How do political parties contribute to democracy? (2022)

Political parties are crucial for the functioning of democracy. They **contest elections**, **form governments**, and **act as the opposition** to hold the government accountable. They help shape public opinion, provide a platform for citizens to participate, and connect the government with the public.

Q15. What is the difference between a national party and a state party in India? (2023)

A party is recognized as a **national party** if it secures at least 6% of the total votes in a Lok Sabha or Assembly election in four states and wins at least four Lok Sabha seats. A **state party** needs to secure at least 6% of the total votes in an Assembly election of a state and win at least two seats.

Q16. What are the major challenges faced by political parties in India? (2022)

Challenges include the **dynastic succession** of leadership, the increasing role of **money and muscle power** in elections, the lack of **internal democracy** within parties, and a general decline in the meaningful choice available to voters.

Q17. Explain the concept of 'secularism' in the Indian context. (2023)

Indian secularism means that the state does not have an official religion and treats all religions equally. The state maintains a principled distance from religion, intervening only when necessary to ensure social harmony or to protect minority rights.

Q18. How does caste play a role in Indian politics? (2022)

Caste influences Indian politics as parties often field candidates from castes dominant in a particular constituency. They appeal to caste sentiments and mobilize voters along caste lines. However, parties must build coalitions across castes to win, so it is not the sole determinant of voting behavior.

Q19. What is the difference between majoritarianism and power sharing? (2022)

Majoritarianism is the belief that the majority community should be able to rule a country in whichever way it wants, disregarding the wishes of the minority. Power sharing is a political arrangement in which power is shared among different social groups to accommodate diversity and ensure stability.

Q20. What is the role of the judiciary in a federal system? (2023)

The judiciary plays a crucial role as an **umpire**. It resolves disputes between the different levels of government in the exercise of their respective powers. The courts have the power to interpret the constitution and the powers of the different levels of government.

Economics

Q21. What is meant by 'development'? How is it measured? (2023)

Development is a comprehensive term that includes economic growth and social well-being. It is measured by various indicators, with the most common being **Per Capita Income**. The **Human Development Index (HDI)** is a broader measure that considers health (life expectancy), education (literacy rate), and living standards.

Q22. Differentiate between the 'primary,' 'secondary,' and 'tertiary' sectors. (2022)

The **primary sector** involves direct use of natural resources (e.g., agriculture). The **secondary sector** transforms raw materials into finished goods (e.g., manufacturing). The **tertiary sector** provides services (e.g., banking, education). These sectors are interdependent, with the tertiary sector supporting the other two.

Q23. What is 'disguised unemployment'? Give an example. (2023)

Disguised unemployment is a situation where more people are employed in a sector than are actually needed, with their contribution to output being zero or negligible. For example, a farming family of seven where only five are needed to cultivate the land. The extra two members are disguisedly unemployed.

Q24. How does the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) supervise the functioning of formal sources of loans? (2022)

The RBI supervises banks to ensure they lend not just to profit-making businesses but also to small borrowers and farmers. It monitors banks' **cash reserve ratio (CRR)** and requires them to submit regular reports on how much they are lending and to whom, ensuring fair lending practices.

Q25. Explain the role of 'money' as a medium of exchange. (2022)

Money acts as a medium of exchange because it can be used to purchase goods and services. It solves the problem of **'double coincidence of wants'**, which is required in a barter system. With money, a buyer doesn't need to find a seller who wants exactly what the buyer has in exchange for what they need.

Q26. What are 'self-help groups' (SHGs)? How do they help the poor? (2023)

SHGs are small groups, mostly of women, who pool their savings and provide small loans to members at a reasonable interest rate. They help the poor by providing an alternative to formal loans, which often require collateral. They also help members become financially self-reliant and create a platform for social and economic empowerment.

Q27. Differentiate between a formal and an informal sector loan. (2022)

**Formal sector loans** are from banks and cooperatives, regulated by the RBI, and have low interest rates and set terms. **Informal sector loans** are from moneylenders and traders, are not regulated, and often have very high interest rates and exploitative terms. The formal sector is preferable as it offers fair and transparent conditions.

Q28. How has globalization affected the Indian economy? (2023)

Globalization has led to increased competition for domestic producers, but also new opportunities. Consumers now have a wider range of products at lower prices. It has created new jobs in sectors like IT and BPO. However, it has also led to job losses in some industries and increased economic inequality.

Q29. What is the main aim of World Trade Organisation (WTO)? (2022)

The main aim of the WTO is to **liberalise international trade**. It establishes a set of rules for global trade and acts as a forum for member nations to negotiate agreements and settle trade disputes. It aims to promote free trade by removing trade barriers.

Q30. What is a consumer right? Name any three consumer rights. (2023)

A consumer right is a legal protection given to a person who purchases goods or services. Three key consumer rights are the **Right to Safety** (to be protected against hazardous goods), the **Right to be Informed** (about the quality, quantity, and price), and the **Right to Choose** (to have a variety of goods and services to choose from).

Geography

Q31. What is 'soil erosion'? Describe two methods of soil conservation. (2023)

Soil erosion is the denudation of the soil cover and subsequent washing down of the topsoil. Two methods of soil conservation are **contour ploughing** (ploughing parallel to the contours of a hill slope to reduce water flow) and **terracing** (creating steps on slopes to control erosion and allow farming).
