The Tale of Custard the Dragon

By Ogden Nash | Class 10 English - First Flight

About the Poem

"The Tale of Custard the Dragon" is a humorous ballad by Ogden Nash about a dragon named Custard who is perceived as cowardly but proves his bravery when real danger appears.

Key Message:

True bravery isn't about appearances but about how one acts when faced with real danger.

Main Characters

  • Belinda: A little girl who lives in a little white house
  • Custard: A dragon who is perceived as cowardly
  • Ink: A little black kitten
  • Blink: A little grey mouse
  • Mustard: A little yellow dog
  • Pirate: The villain who attacks the house

Poem Excerpt

Belinda lived in a little white house,
With a little black kitten and a little grey mouse,
And a little yellow dog and a little red wagon,
And a realio, trulio, little pet dragon.

Now the name of the little black kitten was Ink,
And the little grey mouse, she called her Blink,
And the little yellow dog was sharp as Mustard,
But the dragon was a coward, and she called him Custard.

Custard the dragon had big sharp teeth,
And spikes on top of him and scales underneath,
Mouth like a fireplace, chimney for a nose,
And realio, trulio daggers on his toes.

Summary

Belinda lives with her pets: Ink (kitten), Blink (mouse), Mustard (dog), and Custard (dragon). While the other pets boast about their bravery, Custard is seen as a coward who always cries for a safe cage.

One day, a pirate breaks into the house. All the brave pets run away and hide, but Custard fights the pirate bravely and defeats him. After the danger passes, the other pets claim they would have been braver, but Belinda embraces Custard, and they all live happily together.

Central Theme:

Appearances can be deceptive, and true courage reveals itself in times of real danger.

Important Points

  • The poem is written in ballad form with a rhyming scheme
  • Uses humorous and exaggerated descriptions
  • Challenges stereotypes about bravery and appearance
  • Shows that those who seem weak may be the strongest when needed
  • Highlights the importance of not judging by appearances

Poetic Devices:

Use of humor, exaggeration, repetition ("realio, trulio"), and rhyme scheme to create a lighthearted tone.

About the Poet

Ogden Nash (1902-1971) was an American poet famous for his light verse and unconventional rhymes. He wrote over 500 pieces of comic verse and is known for his humorous and witty poetry.

Vocabulary

  • Coward: A person or animal who lacks courage
  • Pirate: A person who attacks and robs ships at sea
  • Percival: A reference to Sir Percival, a knight of King Arthur's Round Table
  • Spikes: Sharp points
  • Wagon: A vehicle for carrying goods

Moral of the Story

Don't judge others by their appearances or initial impressions. True character is revealed during challenging situations, not through empty boasts.
