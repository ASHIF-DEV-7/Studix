Animals

By Walt Whitman | Class 10 English - First Flight

About the Poem

"Animals" by Walt Whitman is a thoughtful poem that compares animals with human beings. The poet expresses his wish to live with animals because he finds them more peaceful, contented, and self-contained than humans.

Key Message:

Animals possess qualities that humans have lost - they are peaceful, satisfied, and true to their nature.

The Poem

I think I could turn and live with animals, they are so placid and self-contain'd,
I stand and look at them long and long.

They do not sweat and whine about their condition,
They do not lie awake in the dark and weep for their sins,
They do not make me sick discussing their duty to God,

Not one is dissatisfied, not one is demented with the mania of owning things,
Not one kneels to another, nor to his kind that lived thousands of years ago,
Not one is respectable or unhappy over the whole earth.

So they show their relations to me and I accept them,
They bring me tokens of myself, they evince them plainly in their possession
I wonder where they get those tokens,
Did I pass that way huge times ago and negligently drop them?

Summary

The poet expresses his desire to live with animals because they are calm and self-contained. He observes that animals don't complain about their condition, worry about sins, or discuss their duties to God.

Animals are not dissatisfied or obsessed with material possessions. They don't kneel before others or worry about respectability. The poet feels connected to animals and sees in them qualities that humans have lost over time.

Central Theme:

Animals represent the innocence and simplicity that humans have lost in their pursuit of material possessions and social status.

Important Points

  • Animals are content with their lives and don't complain
  • They don't worry about material possessions or social status
  • Animals live in the present without guilt about the past or anxiety about the future
  • The poet sees in animals the qualities that humans once possessed
  • Animals are true to their nature and don't pretend to be what they're not

Poetic Device:

Whitman uses free verse without regular rhyme scheme, reflecting the natural and unrestrained quality of animals.

About the Poet

Walt Whitman (1819-1892) was an American poet, essayist, and journalist, often called the father of free verse. His most famous work is "Leaves of Grass," which celebrates democracy, nature, love, and friendship.

Vocabulary

  • Placid: Not easily upset or excited; calm
  • Self-contain'd: Self-sufficient; not needing others
  • Demented: Mad; insane
  • Mania: Excessive enthusiasm or desire
  • Evince: To reveal the presence of; to show clearly
  • Negligently: Carelessly; without proper attention
