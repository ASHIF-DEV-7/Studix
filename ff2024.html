Chapter-wise Important Questions

1. What was Lencho's main problem in the story "A Letter to God"?
Lencho's main problem was that his entire crop was destroyed by a hailstorm, leaving him and his family with no means of support for the coming year.
2. How did the post office employees help Lencho?
The post office employees collected money from themselves to help Lencho after seeing his unwavering faith in God. They sent him a portion of the money he had requested.
3. Why was Lencho angry when he received the letter?
Lencho was angry because he had received only seventy pesos instead of the hundred he had asked for. He believed that God could never make a mistake and suspected the post office employees had stolen the remaining money.
4. What does Nelson Mandela mean by "courage" in his autobiography?
For Mandela, courage was not the absence of fear but the triumph over it. He believed that brave men are not those who don't feel fear, but those who conquer it.
5. What "twin obligations" does Mandela mention?
Mandela mentions that every man has twin obligations: obligations to his family and obligations to his people, his community, and his country.
6. How did Mandela's understanding of freedom change with age?
As a young boy, Mandela understood freedom as being free to run in the fields. As a student, he wanted freedom to stay out at night. As a young man, he wanted basic freedoms. Later, he understood that true freedom meant freedom for all people.
7. Why does Anne Frank think that "paper has more patience than people"?
Anne Frank believes that paper has more patience than people because she can confide in her diary without fear of judgment or interruption, unlike sharing with people who might grow impatient or dismissive.
8. Why does Anne want to keep a diary?
Anne wants to keep a diary because she feels she doesn't have a true friend she can confide in. She believes that paper has more patience than people and she wants to document her thoughts and experiences.
9. What tells you that Anne loved her grandmother?
Anne writes about her grandmother with deep affection, mentions lighting a candle for her during Christmas, and thinks about her often even after her death, which shows she loved her grandmother dearly.
10. What kind of a girl was Anne Frank?
Anne Frank was intelligent, perceptive, emotional, and thoughtful. She was a good writer who could express her feelings well. She was also sometimes moody and critical but had a deep understanding of human nature.
11. How does Wanda Petronski differ from other children?
Wanda Petronski differs from other children in her quiet nature, her Polish background, her poverty evident from her worn-out blue dress, and her unusual name that sets her apart from others.
12. Why does Maddie stand by and not do anything?
Maddie stands by and does nothing because she doesn't want to lose her popularity and become a target herself. She feels uncomfortable but is too afraid to speak up against Peggy.
13. What important decision does Maddie make?
Maddie decides that she will never stand by silently while someone is being bullied again. She promises herself that if she ever witnesses bullying, she will speak up even if it means losing her friend.
14. What does the Buddha say about death and suffering?
The Buddha says that death and suffering are inevitable parts of life. He teaches that everything that is born will die and that suffering comes from our attachments and desires.
15. Why is Kisa Gotami sad?
Kisa Gotami is sad because her only son has died and she cannot accept his death. She is overwhelmed with grief and wants him to be brought back to life.
16. What lesson does Kisa Gotami learn from the Buddha?
Kisa Gotami learns that death is inevitable and common to all humanity. She understands that grieving cannot bring back the dead and that she must accept the reality of death.
17. How does the poet describe the tiger in the zoo?
The poet describes the tiger in the zoo as a powerful but imprisoned animal. It moves quietly in its cage with quiet rage, ignoring visitors and stalking its limited space.
18. What does the tiger do in the cage?
In the cage, the tiger stalks the length of its cage, ignores visitors, hears patrolling cars at night, and stares at the brilliant stars with his brilliant eyes.
19. How is the tiger in the jungle different from the tiger in the zoo?
The tiger in the jungle is free, powerful, and feared as it hunts for prey near water holes and snarls around villages. In contrast, the zoo tiger is confined, frustrated, and powerless.
20. What is the central idea of the poem "Fire and Ice"?
The central idea of "Fire and Ice" is that human emotions like desire and hatred are powerful enough to destroy the world. Fire represents burning desires while ice represents cold hatred.
21. What does the poet compare fire with?
The poet compares fire with desire, passion, and the emotional intensity that can lead to destruction.
22. According to the poet, what will end the world?
The poet suggests that the world could end in either fire (desire) or ice (hatred), and he believes that both are equally capable of causing destruction.
23. What does the poet say about both ice and fire?
The poet says that he has experienced both desire (fire) and hatred (ice), and he knows that either would be sufficient to destroy the world.
24. Why does the poet want to know where the teachers go?
The child poet wants to know where teachers go after school because he sees them as mysterious figures and wonders if they have normal lives outside of school like other people.
25. What does the poet imagine about his teacher's home life?
The poet imagines that his teacher might live in a typical house, wear casual clothes, watch TV, wash socks, and have parents rather than being a strict teacher all the time.
26. Why is the child curious about his teacher?
The child is curious because he only sees his teacher in the school context as an authority figure and wonders if she has a normal life outside of school like everyone else.
27. What is Amanda's mother constantly nagging her about?
Amanda's mother constantly nags her about sitting properly, cleaning her room, finishing homework, biting nails, and slouching instead of sitting straight.
28. How does Amanda escape from reality?
Amanda escapes from reality by imagining herself in different roles - as a mermaid in a calm sea, an orphan roaming freely, and Rapunzel in a peaceful tower.
29. Why does Amanda wish to be an orphan?
Amanda wishes to be an orphan because she thinks orphans have more freedom to roam and make patterns with their bare feet without anyone nagging them.
30. What does the poet say about the snake in "The Snake Trying"?
The poet describes the snake as beautiful and harmless, trying to escape from being pursued. He suggests that the snake should not be killed as it is graceful and not dangerous.
31. Why does the poet defend the snake?
The poet defends the snake because he believes it is harmless and beautiful. He thinks that all creatures have a right to live and that the snake is merely trying to escape, not attack anyone.
32. What is the theme of the poem "The Snake Trying"?
The theme of the poem is that we should not judge creatures based on preconceived notions and that all living beings have the right to live. It promotes compassion towards all creatures.
33. How does the poet describe the snake's movement?
The poet describes the snake's movement as graceful, curving and looping through the water as it tries to escape from its pursuer.
34. What does the poet want to convey through "The Tale of Custard the Dragon"?
The poet wants to convey that appearances can be deceptive and that true courage isn't about boasting but about acting bravely when needed. Custard appears cowardly but proves to be the bravest when real danger appears.
35. How did everyone perceive Custard the Dragon?
Everyone perceived Custard as a coward because he always cried for a nice safe cage while the other pets boasted about their bravery.
36. What happened when the pirate arrived?
When the pirate arrived, all the pets who had boasted about their bravery ran away and hid in fear. Only Custard, who was perceived as cowardly, attacked the pirate and saved everyone.
37. How does the poem challenge traditional notions of bravery?
The poem challenges traditional notions of bravery by showing that the one who seemed most cowardly was actually the bravest when real danger appeared, while those who boasted about their courage were the first to run away.
38. What is the Fog compared to in the poem?
The fog is compared to a cat that moves silently on its little feet, sits looking over the city and harbor, and then moves on silently.
39. How does the fog come according to the poet?
According to the poet, the fog comes silently and stealthily like a cat, on its little cat feet.
40. What does the fog do over the city and harbor?
The fog sits looking over the city and harbor like a cat sitting on its haunches, watching everything silently before moving on.
