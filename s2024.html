Section A - Physics Multiple Choice Questions (1 mark each)
Q1. 1 Mark

The SI unit of electric current is:

  1. Volt
  2. Ampere
  3. Coulomb
  4. Ohm
Answer: (b) Ampere
Explanation: The SI base unit for electric current is ampere (A), named after André-Marie Ampère.
Q2. 1 Mark

A concave mirror produces a real image when the object is placed:

  1. At the focus
  2. Between focus and pole
  3. Beyond the center of curvature
  4. At infinity
Answer: (c) Beyond the center of curvature
Explanation: When object is placed beyond center of curvature, concave mirror forms real, inverted, and diminished image.
Q3. 1 Mark

The power of a lens is measured in:

  1. Meter
  2. Dioptre
  3. Watt
  4. Newton
Answer: (b) Dioptre
Explanation: Power of lens = 1/focal length (in meters). Unit is dioptre (D).
Q4. 1 Mark

Fleming's left-hand rule is used to find:

  1. Direction of induced current
  2. Direction of magnetic field
  3. Direction of force on current-carrying conductor
  4. Direction of electric field
Answer: (c) Direction of force on current-carrying conductor
Explanation: Fleming's left-hand rule gives direction of force when current flows through conductor in magnetic field.

Section B - Physics Short Answer Questions (2 marks each)
Q13. 2 Marks

State Ohm's law and write its mathematical expression.

Answer:
Ohm's Law: The current flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference across its ends, provided the temperature and other physical conditions remain constant.
V = IR
Where V = Voltage, I = Current, R = Resistance
Q14. 2 Marks

What is the difference between real and virtual images?

Answer:
Real Image:
  • Can be obtained on a screen
  • Formed by actual intersection of light rays
  • Always inverted
Virtual Image:
  • Cannot be obtained on a screen
  • Formed by apparent intersection of light rays
  • Always erect
Q15. 2 Marks

Define electromagnetic induction and state Faraday's first law.

Answer:
Electromagnetic Induction: The phenomenon of inducing an EMF (and hence current) in a conductor when it is placed in a changing magnetic field.

Faraday's First Law: Whenever there is a change in magnetic flux linked with a coil, an EMF is induced in the coil.
Q16. 2 Marks

Calculate the resistance of a wire of length 2m, cross-sectional area 2×10⁻⁶ m², and resistivity 1.7×10⁻⁸ Ωm.

Answer:
Given: L = 2m, A = 2×10⁻⁶ m², ρ = 1.7×10⁻⁸ Ωm
R = ρL/A
R = (1.7×10⁻⁸ × 2)/(2×10⁻⁶)
R = 3.4×10⁻⁸/2×10⁻⁶
R = 0.017 Ω
Section B - Chemistry Short Answer Questions (2 marks each)
Q17. 2 Marks

What are isotopes? Give an example.

Answer:
Isotopes: Atoms of the same element having the same atomic number but different mass numbers (different number of neutrons).

Example: Carbon has three isotopes:
  • Carbon-12 (⁶₁₂C) - 6 protons, 6 neutrons
  • Carbon-13 (⁶₁₃C) - 6 protons, 7 neutrons
  • Carbon-14 (⁶₁₄C) - 6 protons, 8 neutrons
Q18. 2 Marks

Balance the chemical equation: Al + O₂ → Al₂O₃

Answer:
Unbalanced: Al + O₂ → Al₂O₃
Balanced:
4Al + 3O₂ → 2Al₂O₃
Check:
  • Al: 4 atoms on both sides
  • O: 6 atoms on both sides
Q19. 2 Marks

Define acids and bases according to Arrhenius theory.

Answer:
Arrhenius Acid: A substance that produces hydrogen ions (H⁺) when dissolved in water.
Example: HCl → H⁺ + Cl⁻

Arrhenius Base: A substance that produces hydroxide ions (OH⁻) when dissolved in water.
Example: NaOH → Na⁺ + OH⁻
Q20. 2 Marks

What is the difference between saturated and unsaturated hydrocarbons?

Answer:
Saturated Hydrocarbons (Alkanes):
  • Contain only single bonds between carbon atoms
  • General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂
  • Example: Methane (CH₄)
Unsaturated Hydrocarbons:
  • Contain double or triple bonds between carbon atoms
  • Alkenes (C=C): CₙH₂ₙ, Alkynes (C≡C): CₙH₂ₙ₋₂
  • Example: Ethene (C₂H₄), Ethyne (C₂H₂)
Section B - Biology Short Answer Questions (2 marks each)
Q21. 2 Marks

What is the difference between aerobic and anaerobic respiration?

Answer:
Aerobic Respiration:
  • Requires oxygen
  • Complete breakdown of glucose
  • Produces 38 ATP molecules
  • End products: CO₂ and H₂O
Anaerobic Respiration:
  • Does not require oxygen
  • Incomplete breakdown of glucose
  • Produces 2 ATP molecules
  • End products: Lactic acid or ethanol + CO₂
Q22. 2 Marks

Define heredity and variation.

Answer:
Heredity: The transmission of characters or traits from parents to offspring through genes. It explains why offspring resemble their parents.

Variation: The differences in characters or traits among individuals of the same species. It is the basis for evolution and natural selection.

Section C - Physics Short Answer Questions (3 marks each)
Q24. 3 Marks

Derive the relation between power, voltage, and resistance in an electric circuit.

Answer:
Power in electric circuit: P = VI
Using Ohm's law: V = IR
Substituting: P = (IR) × I = I²R

Also, I = V/R
Substituting: P = V × (V/R) = V²/R

Three expressions for power:
P = VI = I²R = V²/R
Where P = Power (Watts), V = Voltage (Volts), I = Current (Amperes), R = Resistance (Ohms)
Q25. 3 Marks

Explain the working principle of an electric motor.

Answer:
Principle: Electric motor works on the principle that a current-carrying conductor experiences a force when placed in a magnetic field.

Working:
  • Current flows through the coil ABCD
  • Fleming's left-hand rule determines force direction
  • Forces on AB and CD are equal and opposite
  • This creates a torque, rotating the coil
  • Commutator reverses current direction every half rotation
  • This ensures continuous rotation in the same direction

Section C - Chemistry Short Answer Questions (3 marks each)
Q26. 3 Marks

Explain the formation of ionic bonds with an example.

Answer:
Ionic Bond Formation: Ionic bonds are formed by the complete transfer of electrons from metal atoms to non-metal atoms.

Example: Formation of NaCl
  • Sodium (Na): Electronic configuration = 2,8,1
  • Chlorine (Cl): Electronic configuration = 2,8,7
  • Na loses 1 electron → Na⁺ (2,8)
  • Cl gains 1 electron → Cl⁻ (2,8,8)
  • Electrostatic attraction between Na⁺ and Cl⁻ forms NaCl
Na → Na⁺ + e⁻
Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻
Na⁺ + Cl⁻ → NaCl
Q27. 3 Marks

What are functional groups? Give examples of three functional groups with their structures.

Answer:
Functional Groups: Specific groups of atoms that determine the chemical properties and reactions of organic compounds.

Examples:
1. Hydroxyl group (-OH): Found in alcohols
2. Carboxyl group (-COOH): Found in carboxylic acids
3. Amino group (-NH₂): Found in amines
These groups are responsible for the characteristic properties and reactions of their respective compound classes.

Q28. 3 Marks

Describe the structure and function of chloroplast.

Answer:
Structure:
  • Double membrane: Outer and inner membrane
  • Stroma: Fluid-filled space containing enzymes
  • Thylakoids: Disc-like structures containing chlorophyll
  • Grana: Stacks of thylakoids
Function:

  • Converts CO₂ and H₂O into glucose using sunlight
Q29. 3 Marks

Explain the process of natural selection with an example.

Answer:
Natural Selection: The process by which organisms with favorable traits survive and reproduce more successfully than others.

Steps:
  • Variation exists in population
  • Struggle for survival
  • Survival of the fittest
  • Favorable traits passed to offspring
Example: Industrial melanism in peppered moths
  • Light-colored moths on light tree bark (before pollution)
  • Industrial pollution darkened tree bark
  • Dark moths had survival advantage
  • Dark moth population increased over time

Answer:
[Ray diagram showing object between F and P, with virtual, erect, magnified image formed behind mirror]
Image characteristics: Virtual, erect, magnified

Derivation of Mirror Formula:
Consider a concave mirror with object AB at distance u from pole P
Image A'B' is formed at distance v from pole P

From similar triangles and geometry:
1/f = 1/u + 1/v
Where:
f = focal length
u = object distance
v = image distance

Sign Convention:
  • Distances measured from pole
  • Real distances are positive
  • Virtual distances are negative
  • Heights above axis are positive

Section D - Chemistry Long Answer Questions (5 marks each)
Q32. 5 Marks

Describe the modern periodic table. Explain the periodic trends in atomic size and metallic character.

Answer:
Modern Periodic Table:
  • Based on atomic number (Mendeleev used atomic mass)
  • 118 elements arranged in 7 periods and 18 groups
  • Elements in same group have same valence electrons
  • Elements in same period have same number of shells
Periodic Trends:

1. Atomic Size:
  • Across period: Decreases left to right
  • Reason: Nuclear charge increases, electron shells remain same
  • Down group: Increases top to bottom
  • Reason: Number of electron shells increases
2. Metallic Character:
  • Across period: Decreases left to right
  • Reason: Tendency to lose electrons decreases
  • Down group: Increases top to bottom
  • Reason: Atomic size increases, easier to lose outer electrons
Examples:
  • Period 3: Na > Mg > Al > Si > P > S > Cl (metallic character)
  • Group 1: Li < Na < K < Rb < Cs (atomic size)
Q33. 5 Marks

What are hydrocarbons? Classify them with examples. Write the properties and uses of ethanol.

Answer:
Hydrocarbons: Organic compounds containing only carbon and hydrogen atoms.

Classification:
1. Saturated Hydrocarbons (Alkanes):
  • Only single bonds between carbon atoms
  • General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂
  • Examples: Methane (CH₄), Ethane (C₂H₆), Propane (C₃H₈)
2. Unsaturated Hydrocarbons:
  • Alkenes: One or more C=C double bonds, CₙH₂ₙ
  • Example: Ethene (C₂H₄), Propene (C₃H₆)
  • Alkynes: One or more C≡C triple bonds, CₙH₂ₙ₋₂
  • Example: Ethyne (C₂H₂), Propyne (C₃H₄)
Ethanol (C₂H₅OH) Properties:
  • Colorless liquid at room temperature
  • Pleasant smell and burning taste
  • Boiling point: 78°C
  • Soluble in water in all proportions
  • Neutral to litmus
  • Burns with blue flame
Uses of Ethanol:
  • Alcoholic beverages
  • Antiseptic and disinfectant
  • Solvent in medicines and cosmetics
  • Fuel (gasohol - petrol + ethanol)
  • Manufacturing of ethanoic acid
Q34. 5 Marks

Describe the human respiratory system. Explain the process of breathing and gaseous exchange.

Answer:
Human Respiratory System:
Structure:
  • Nasal cavity: Filters, warms, and moistens air
  • Pharynx: Common passage for air and food
  • Larynx: Voice box with vocal cords
  • Trachea: Windpipe supported by cartilage rings
  • Bronchi: Two tubes entering each lung
  • Bronchioles: Smaller branches within lungs
  • Alveoli: Tiny air sacs for gas exchange
  • Diaphragm: Muscular sheet below lungs
Process of Breathing:
Inspiration (Inhalation):
  • Diaphragm contracts and moves down
  • Rib cage moves up and outward
  • Lung volume increases, pressure decreases
  • Air rushes into lungs
Expiration (Exhalation):
  • Diaphragm relaxes and moves up
  • Rib cage moves down and inward
  • Lung volume decreases, pressure increases
  • Air is pushed out of lungs
Gaseous Exchange:
  • Occurs in alveoli by diffusion
  • Oxygen moves from alveoli to blood
  • Carbon dioxide moves from blood to alveoli
  • Large surface area and thin walls facilitate exchange
  • Hemoglobin transports oxygen in blood
Q35. 5 Marks

Explain Mendel's laws of inheritance with examples. What is the difference between dominant and recessive traits?

Answer:
Mendel's Laws of Inheritance:

1. Law of Dominance:
  • In a pair of contrasting characters, one is dominant over the other
  • Dominant trait is expressed in heterozygous condition
  • Recessive trait is expressed only in homozygous condition
  • Example: Tall (T) is dominant over dwarf (t) in pea plants
2. Law of Segregation:
  • Each parent has two factors (alleles) for each character
  • Only one factor is passed to each gamete
  • Factors segregate during gamete formation
  • Example: Tt × Tt → TT, Tt, Tt, tt (1:2:1 ratio)
3. Law of Independent Assortment:
  • Different characters are inherited independently
  • Applies when genes are on different chromosomes
  • Example: Shape and color of seeds segregate independently
  • Dihybrid cross gives 9:3:3:1 phenotypic ratio
Dominant vs Recessive Traits:
Dominant Traits:
  • Expressed in both homozygous (TT) and heterozygous (Tt) conditions
  • Represented by capital letters (T, R, B)
  • Examples: Brown eyes, curly hair, presence of widow's peak
Recessive Traits:
  • Expressed only in homozygous condition (tt)
  • Represented by small letters (t, r, b)
  • Examples: Blue eyes, straight hair, absence of widow's peak
Example Cross:
Tall (TT) × Dwarf (tt)
F₁: All Tt (Tall)
F₂: TT, Tt, Tt, tt → 3 Tall : 1 Dwarf
