The Ball Poem

By John Berryman | Class 10 English - First Flight

About the Poem

"The Ball Poem" by John Berryman is a poignant reflection on loss and the process of growing up. The poem describes a young boy who loses his ball and learns an important lesson about responsibility and the nature of loss.

Key Message:

The poem teaches us that loss is an inevitable part of life, and we must learn to accept it and move on.

The Poem

What is the boy now, who has lost his ball,
What, what is he to do? I saw it go
Merrily bouncing, down the street, and then
Merrily overâ€”there it is in the water!
No use to say 'O there are other balls':

An ultimate shaking grief fixes the boy
As he stands rigid, trembling, staring down
All his young days into the harbour where
His ball went. I would not intrude on him,
A dime, another ball, is worthless.

Now he senses first responsibility
In a world of possessions. People will take
Balls, balls will be lost always, little boy.
And no one buys a ball back. Money is external.

He is learning, well behind his desperate eyes,
The epistemology of loss, how to stand up
Knowing what every man must one day know
And most know many days, how to stand up.

Summary

The poem begins with a boy who loses his ball while playing. The ball bounces away and lands in the water. The boy is deeply affected by this loss and stands frozen, grieving for his lost possession.

The poet reflects that offering the boy money or a new ball would be meaningless. This experience teaches the boy about responsibility and the nature of loss in a world where possessions come and go.

Central Theme:

Learning to cope with loss and understanding that some things in life cannot be replaced.

Important Points

  • The boy's ball represents childhood innocence and simple joys
  • Loss of the ball symbolizes the boy's first encounter with responsibility
  • The poet emphasizes that money cannot replace emotional attachments
  • The poem shows how painful experiences contribute to personal growth
  • "Epistemology of loss" means understanding the nature of loss through experience

Symbolism:

The ball symbolizes childhood, innocence, and the things we cherish but eventually lose as we grow up.

About the Poet

John Berryman (1914-1972) was an American poet and scholar, known for his confessional style of poetry. He won the Pulitzer Prize for poetry in 1965 for his collection "77 Dream Songs."

Vocabulary

  • Epistemology: The theory of knowledge, especially regarding its methods, validity, and scope
  • Intrude: To interrupt or interfere without invitation
  • External: Coming from outside; not intrinsic
  • Possessions: Things owned or possessed
