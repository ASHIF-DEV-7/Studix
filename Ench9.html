Class 10 English –
“The Proposal” by Anton Chekhov

Introduction (10 Lines)

1. “The Proposal” is a humorous one-act play by Russian playwright Anton Chekhov.

2. It revolves around a marriage proposal between Lomov and Natalya.

3. The story highlights the petty quarrels of landowning families.

4. Chekhov uses comedy to reveal human weaknesses like greed and pride.

5. The play is set in the Russian countryside in the late 19th century.

6. Characters include Ivan Vassilevitch Lomov, Natalya Stepanovna, and her father Stepan Chubukov.

7. The central theme is the absurdity of human behavior during serious occasions.

8. Instead of romance, the proposal turns into heated arguments.

9. Chekhov mocks the social customs of arranged marriages.

10. The play ends with a marriage but without genuine love or understanding.

Explanation – 20 Points

  • 1. Lomov visits Chubukov’s house to ask for Natalya’s hand in marriage.
  • 2. He is dressed formally and appears nervous and hesitant.
  • 3. Chubukov warmly welcomes Lomov, guessing his purpose.
  • 4. Lomov tries to talk about marriage but digresses repeatedly.
  • 5. He begins a conversation about a disputed piece of land called Oxen Meadows.
  • 6. Natalya enters, and a heated argument starts over the ownership of the Meadows.
  • 7. Both families claim the land belongs to them.
  • 8. The quarrel escalates, and Lomov becomes agitated.
  • 9. Chubukov joins the argument, supporting his daughter.
  • 10. Lomov, already weak-hearted, feels faint but refuses to leave.
  • 11. After Lomov exits, Chubukov informs Natalya of Lomov’s proposal intention.
  • 12. Natalya panics, realizing she may lose the chance of marriage.
  • 13. She calls Lomov back immediately.
  • 14. When Lomov returns, she changes the topic and acts politely.
  • 15. Soon another quarrel begins about whose hunting dog is superior—Guess or Squeezer.
  • 16. Lomov, already anxious, collapses on a chair, almost unconscious.
  • 17. Chubukov splashes water and revives him.
  • 18. Despite continuous quarrels, Chubukov quickly announces their engagement.
  • 19. The couple continues to argue even after the engagement is confirmed.
  • 20. The play ends humorously, showing marriage as a social contract rather than a romantic union.

Conclusion (7–8 Lines)

1. Anton Chekhov’s play reveals the trivial reasons behind major life decisions.

2. Instead of love, property and pride dominate the marriage discussion.

3. The continuous quarrels show the irrational side of human nature.

4. Marriage, in this story, becomes a mere social arrangement.

5. The ending proves that people often value status and land over emotions.

6. Humor is used to point out serious flaws in society.

7. Chekhov leaves readers amused but thoughtful about human follies.

8. The play remains timeless for its satire on greed and vanity.

Moral of the Story

The moral: Human pride, greed, and social status often overshadow genuine feelings. Relationships should be built on love and understanding, not on wealth, land, or superficial arguments.
