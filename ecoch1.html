Development Economics

Chapter 1 - Class 10 Complete Notes

1. Introduction to Development

Development: Development refers to the progressive changes in the economy, society and polity that lead to improvement in the quality of life of people.

What is Development?

Development is a multidimensional concept that encompasses not just economic growth but also improvements in health, education, equality, and overall well-being of people. It involves positive changes that make life better for individuals and communities.

Key Aspects of Development:

  • Economic Growth: Increase in production and income
  • Social Progress: Improvements in health, education, and living standards
  • Political Freedom: Democratic rights and participation
  • Environmental Sustainability: Protecting natural resources for future generations
  • Equity: Fair distribution of benefits across all sections of society

Different People, Different Goals

Development means different things to different people based on their circumstances, needs, and aspirations. What may be development for one person might not be the same for another.

Examples:

  • For a landless farmer: Getting land, better seeds, and irrigation facilities
  • For an urban unemployed youth: Getting a job with decent wages
  • For a girl child: Access to education and freedom from discrimination
  • For industrialists: More profits and expansion opportunities
  • For environmentalists: Sustainable use of natural resources

2. Income and Other Goals

Importance of Income

Income is undoubtedly an important component of development as it provides the means to fulfill basic needs like food, clothing, shelter, education, and healthcare. Higher income generally leads to better living standards.

Why Income Matters:

  • Enables purchase of basic necessities
  • Provides access to better education and healthcare
  • Improves quality of housing and living conditions
  • Offers more choices and opportunities
  • Enhances social status and respect

Beyond Income - Non-Material Goals

However, income alone cannot determine development. People also seek non-material things that are equally important for a fulfilling life.

Non-Material Aspects of Development:

  • Security: Personal and economic security
  • Respect and Dignity: Social recognition and equality
  • Freedom: Political and personal freedoms
  • Clean Environment: Pollution-free air, water, and surroundings
  • Peace: Absence of conflict and violence
  • Equal Treatment: Non-discrimination based on gender, caste, religion
  • Democratic Rights: Right to vote, express opinions, participate in governance

Real-life Example:

Consider two states in India - Punjab and Kerala. Punjab has higher per capita income than Kerala, but Kerala has better literacy rates, lower infant mortality, and higher life expectancy. This shows that development is not just about income but also about social indicators.

3. National Development

What is National Development?

National development refers to the overall progress of a country in various spheres - economic, social, political, and environmental. It aims to improve the standard of living of all citizens.

National Development: The comprehensive growth of a nation that includes economic prosperity, social well-being, political stability, and environmental sustainability for all its citizens.

Challenges in Defining National Development

Since different people have different developmental goals, it becomes challenging to define what constitutes national development. What might be development for one group might be harmful to another.

Conflicting Developmental Goals:

  • Dam Construction: Provides electricity and irrigation (beneficial for urban areas and farmers) but displaces tribal communities
  • Industrial Development: Creates jobs and economic growth but may cause environmental pollution
  • Mining: Generates revenue and employment but destroys forests and affects local communities

Criteria for National Development

For national development, we need to think about what would be the best for the largest number of people. The development goals that are beneficial for the majority should be given priority.

Principles for National Development:

  • Inclusive Growth: Benefits should reach all sections of society
  • Sustainable Development: Meet present needs without compromising future generations
  • Equity: Fair distribution of resources and opportunities
  • Participatory Development: Involvement of people in decision-making
  • Balanced Development: Growth across all sectors and regions

4. How to Compare Different Countries or States?

Need for Comparison

To understand the level of development, we need to compare different countries or states. This helps in identifying successful development models and learning from best practices.

Income as a Development Indicator

Income is the most commonly used criterion for comparing development levels. Countries with higher income are generally considered more developed.

Why Income is Used for Comparison:

  • Easy to measure and quantify
  • Available for most countries
  • Reflects economic prosperity
  • Enables purchase of essential goods and services
  • Generally correlates with better living standards

Per Capita Income

Per Capita Income: The average income earned by each person in a country or state during a particular year.
Per Capita Income = Total National Income ÷ Total Population

Per capita income is calculated by dividing the total income of the country by its total population. It gives us an average figure of how much each person would earn if the income were distributed equally.

Example Calculation:

If India's total national income is ₹150 lakh crores and population is 130 crores, then:

Per Capita Income = ₹150 lakh crores ÷ 130 crores = ₹1,15,385 per person per year

World Bank Classification

The World Bank classifies countries based on per capita income into different categories:

Category Per Capita Income (2019) Examples
Low Income Countries $1,045 or less Afghanistan, Chad, Mali
Lower Middle Income $1,046 to $4,095 India, Bangladesh, Kenya
Upper Middle Income $4,096 to $12,695 China, Brazil, South Africa
High Income Countries $12,696 or more USA, Germany, Japan

5. Income and Other Criteria - Limitations

Limitations of Per Capita Income

While per capita income is widely used, it has several limitations as a measure of development:

Problems with Per Capita Income:

  • Inequality: It doesn't show how income is distributed among people
  • Average Bias: High income of few people can inflate the average
  • Non-monetary factors: Ignores non-material aspects of life
  • Quality of life: Doesn't reflect health, education, environment
  • Purchasing power: Same income may buy different things in different countries

Example - Kerala vs Punjab:

Indicator Kerala Punjab
Per Capita Income (2018-19) ₹2,04,105 ₹2,58,351
Literacy Rate 93.9% 76.7%
Infant Mortality Rate 7 per 1000 21 per 1000
Life Expectancy 75 years 72 years

Despite lower per capita income, Kerala performs better on social indicators, showing that income alone doesn't determine development.

Need for Other Indicators

Due to limitations of income as a sole indicator, we need other measures that capture different dimensions of development:

Important Social Indicators:

  • Health Indicators: Life expectancy, infant mortality rate, maternal mortality rate
  • Education Indicators: Literacy rate, school enrollment, dropout rates
  • Gender Indicators: Sex ratio, women's participation in workforce
  • Environmental Indicators: Forest cover, pollution levels
  • Infrastructure: Access to electricity, clean water, sanitation

6. Human Development Index (HDI)

What is Human Development Index?

Human Development Index (HDI): A composite index developed by the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP) that measures the average achievement of a country in three basic dimensions of human development.

Components of HDI

HDI considers three key dimensions of human development:

Three Dimensions of HDI:

  • Health: Measured by life expectancy at birth
  • Education: Measured by mean years of schooling and expected years of schooling
  • Standard of Living: Measured by Gross National Income (GNI) per capita

HDI Scale and Classification

HDI is measured on a scale of 0 to 1, where:

  • 0.800 and above: Very High Human Development
  • 0.700 - 0.799: High Human Development
  • 0.550 - 0.699: Medium Human Development
  • Below 0.550: Low Human Development

HDI Rankings (2020):

Rank Country HDI Value
1 Norway 0.957
2 Ireland 0.955
3 Switzerland 0.955
131 India 0.645

Advantages of HDI

Why HDI is Better than Per Capita Income:

  • Considers multiple dimensions of development
  • Includes health and education along with income
  • Provides a more holistic view of development
  • Helps in identifying areas that need attention
  • Enables better comparison between countries

Limitations of HDI

Criticisms of HDI:

  • Doesn't consider inequality within countries
  • Ignores environmental sustainability
  • No consideration of political freedom and human rights
  • Doesn't account for cultural and social factors
  • Equal weightage to all three dimensions may not be appropriate

7. Sustainability of Development

What is Sustainable Development?

Sustainable Development: Development that meets the needs of the present generation without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.

Need for Sustainable Development

The rapid industrialization and economic growth have led to overexploitation of natural resources and environmental degradation. This threatens the well-being of future generations.

Environmental Problems:

  • Resource Depletion: Exhaustion of coal, petroleum, minerals, groundwater
  • Pollution: Air, water, and soil pollution affecting health
  • Climate Change: Global warming due to greenhouse gas emissions
  • Deforestation: Loss of biodiversity and ecological balance
  • Land Degradation: Soil erosion and desertification

Principles of Sustainable Development

Key Principles:

  • Conservation: Judicious use of natural resources
  • Renewable Energy: Shift from fossil fuels to clean energy
  • Recycling: Reuse and recycle materials to reduce waste
  • Population Control: Maintaining optimal population size
  • Technology: Development of environment-friendly technologies
  • Awareness: Education about environmental protection

Examples of Sustainable Practices:

  • Renewable Energy: Solar panels, wind energy, hydroelectric power
  • Organic Farming: Chemical-free agriculture
  • Rainwater Harvesting: Conservation of groundwater
  • Public Transport: Reducing vehicular pollution
  • Afforestation: Planting trees to maintain forest cover

Groundwater: A Case Study

Groundwater is a perfect example of how overuse of natural resources can lead to sustainability issues:

Groundwater Crisis in India:

  • Rapid depletion due to excessive extraction
  • Green Revolution led to increased use of tube wells
  • Water table falling in many states like Punjab, Haryana, Rajasthan
  • Quality deterioration due to contamination
  • Inter-state disputes over water sharing

Solutions for Sustainable Development

Strategies for Sustainability:

  • Policy Changes: Environmental laws and regulations
  • International Cooperation: Global agreements like Paris Climate Accord
  • Individual Actions: Lifestyle changes and responsible consumption
  • Corporate Responsibility: Businesses adopting green practices
  • Innovation: Development of clean technologies
  • Education: Creating awareness about environmental issues

8. Chapter Summary

Key Takeaways:

  • Development is a multidimensional concept that goes beyond economic growth
  • Different people have different developmental goals based on their circumstances
  • Income is important but not the only indicator of development
  • Per capita income has limitations and doesn't show the complete picture
  • Human Development Index provides a better measure by including health and education
  • Sustainable development is crucial for the welfare of future generations
  • Environmental protection and resource conservation are essential for long-term development
  • National development should be inclusive, equitable, and sustainable

Important Terms to Remember:

  • Development: Progressive changes leading to improvement in quality of life
  • Per Capita Income: Average income per person in a country
  • Human Development Index: Composite measure of health, education, and income
  • Sustainable Development: Development without compromising future needs
  • Literacy Rate: Percentage of population that can read and write
  • Infant Mortality Rate: Number of infant deaths per 1000 live births
  • Life Expectancy: Average number of years a person is expected to live
